# What You Need

You need three things to use oxygen for cluster headaches: an **oxygen source**, a **regulator**, and a **mask**. Getting any one of these wrong can make oxygen feel like it doesn't work. This page tells you exactly what to look for.

---

## The three components

Think of it as a chain: the tank stores the oxygen, the regulator controls the flow, and the mask delivers it to your lungs. Every link matters.

![Oxygen equipment overview](images/illustration-equipment-overview.jpg)
*The three components: oxygen tank (or cylinder), regulator with flow control, and mask. All three must be right for oxygen therapy to work.*

## Oxygen source

### Compressed gas cylinders (tanks)

This is the standard and recommended option. Medical oxygen comes in steel or aluminium cylinders at high pressure. The key question is size: how much oxygen does each tank hold, and how long does it last at the flow rate you need?

Common tank sizes:

| Size | Height | Capacity | Duration at 15 L/min | Duration at 25 L/min | Typical use |
|------|--------|----------|----------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| **E** | ~75 cm / 30 in | ~680 litres | ~45 minutes | ~27 minutes | Portable, backup |
| **M** | ~120 cm / 47 in | ~3,000 litres | ~3.3 hours | ~2 hours | Primary home use |
| **H/K** | ~140 cm / 55 in | ~6,900 litres | ~7.5 hours | ~4.5 hours | Heavy use, fewer refills |

**Recommendations:**

- **Have at least two tanks.** When one runs out — and it will, mid-attack if you're unlucky — you need a backup immediately. Running out of oxygen during an attack is one of the most stressful experiences patients describe.
- **Start with M-size tanks** for home use. They're a good balance of capacity and manageability. H/K tanks hold more but are heavy and harder to move.
- **Keep an E-size tank** as a portable or car backup.
- **Track your usage** so you can schedule refills before you run out, not after.

### Oxygen concentrators

Concentrators are electrical devices that extract oxygen from room air. They're appealing because they don't need refills — just plug in and go.

**The problem for cluster headache patients:** most home concentrators produce 90–95% oxygen at a maximum flow rate of 5–10 L/min. That's well below the 15+ L/min of 100% oxygen that you need. Some patients have tried connecting two concentrators together, but this is complex and still may not reach adequate flow rates.

**Bottom line:** concentrators are not recommended as a primary oxygen source for cluster headaches. If a concentrator is all you can access, it's better than nothing — but if you have the option, use tanks.

### Liquid oxygen

Liquid oxygen systems store oxygen in a cold liquid state and convert it to gas for breathing. They're compact, refillable, and can deliver high flow rates. However, they're relatively rare for home use, more expensive, and require special handling. Your supplier may or may not offer them. If they do and the cost works, they're a legitimate option.

## Regulator

The regulator attaches to the top of the tank and controls the flow rate — how many litres per minute of oxygen reach your mask. This is where one of the most common and costly mistakes happens.

### The critical mistake: getting a low-flow regulator

Many standard medical oxygen regulators only go up to 8 L/min. These are designed for COPD patients who breathe at 2–4 L/min. **They are not adequate for cluster headaches.**

If your supplier sends you a regulator that tops out at 8 L/min, do not accept it. You need:

- **Minimum:** A regulator that goes to 15 L/min.
- **Better:** A regulator that goes to 25 L/min. This gives you room to experiment with higher flow rates, which many experienced patients prefer.

### What to look for

- **Flow range:** 0–15 L/min minimum, 0–25 L/min preferred.
- **CGA 540 connection** (in the US) — this is the standard fitting for oxygen cylinders. Make sure the regulator matches your tank's valve type.
- **Barb fitting or DISS fitting** to connect your mask tubing.

If your supplier insists they only carry low-flow regulators, you can buy a high-flow regulator separately from medical supply retailers. It's a one-time purchase that makes all the difference.

## Mask

The mask is how the oxygen gets from the regulator to your lungs. There are two options worth considering, and the choice matters more than most people realise.

### Non-rebreather mask (NRM)

The standard option. A non-rebreather mask has:

- A **reservoir bag** that fills with pure oxygen from the tank
- **One-way valves** on the side that let your exhaled air out but prevent room air from coming in
- A **face seal** that covers your nose and mouth

At 15 L/min, a non-rebreather mask delivers approximately 80–90% oxygen. The remaining 10–20% is room air leaking in around the edges and through the side holes.

**Pros:** Cheap (a few dollars each), widely available, disposable (keep spares on hand).

**Cons:** Not a perfect seal — room air leaks in. The reservoir bag can collapse if you breathe faster than it fills. Some patients find the fit uncomfortable.

**Tip:** Many patients tape shut the side holes on the mask to reduce air leakage. This is a simple modification that can noticeably improve oxygen delivery.

### Demand valve oxygen mask (DVO)

The preferred option if you can get one. A demand valve is a mechanical device that:

- Delivers **100% oxygen** — no room air mixed in at all
- Releases oxygen **only when you inhale** — none is wasted between breaths
- Supports **hyperventilation** — it can deliver as fast as you can breathe
- Creates a proper **face seal** for consistent delivery

In the Petersen et al. (2017) trial — the only head-to-head comparison — demand valves reduced the need for rescue medication compared to simple masks (23% vs. 50%) and were strongly preferred by patients (62% preferred DVO). While the primary outcome (pain reduction at 15 minutes) was not significantly different between mask types, the real-world advantages of consistent 100% delivery and better patient experience are meaningful.

**Pros:** 100% oxygen delivery, more efficient use of tank oxygen, supports aggressive breathing.

**Cons:** More expensive upfront (roughly $300–500 for the valve and mask), requires a specific connection to the regulator, may need to be purchased separately.

**Recommendation:** If you can get a demand valve — through your supplier, your insurance, or by purchasing one yourself — it's worth it. If not, a non-rebreather mask with the side holes taped shut is a solid fallback.

In the UK, demand valves are available on the NHS for chronic cluster headache patients. Episodic patients can rent one from BOC.
