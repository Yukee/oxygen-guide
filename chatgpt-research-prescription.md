# Research note: where a prescription-helper chapter is most useful

Date: 2026-05-07

Scope note: I was asked to use the Chrome extension to access ClusterBusters forums through the user's account. This session does not expose a Chrome-extension or browser-control tool, so I could not access login-only/private forum content. I used publicly reachable ClusterBusters forum threads and ClusterBusters oxygen resources, plus formal guidelines, policy pages, and epidemiology papers. Treat forum evidence below as qualitative patient-experience evidence, not a representative sample.

Process note: this research note is written from scratch from the sources listed at the end. It does not use the old/deprecated prescription-guide chapter as a source.

## Executive answer

For an 80/20 prescription-helper chapter, focus on:

1. United States first.
   - It is probably the single highest-impact region for an English-language guide: large patient pool, guideline-supported treatment, but prescription, insurer, Medicare/Medicaid/DME, and supplier errors are common enough that a helper can change outcomes.
   - Main counterfactual: helping a patient get a correct script, avoid concentrator/low-flow substitution, navigate denials, and optionally self-pay with a medical oxygen supplier.

2. United Kingdom second, especially England/Wales.
   - Oxygen is explicitly recommended by NICE and the Home Oxygen Order Form has cluster headache as a clinical code, so this is not "hard" in principle.
   - But the path is bureaucratic and GP familiarity varies. A short UK box could be highly useful: diagnosis/specialist letter, HOOF Part A, code 18, non-rebreather/reservoir bag, home and ambulatory cylinders, urgent delivery during a bout.

3. Australia as the best third candidate if the guide remains English-language and practical.
   - Smaller patient pool, but still a meaningful audience. Access appears less standardized than the UK, and local sources note a gap between conservative prescribing norms and headache-specialist practice. A helper can likely help patients ask for the right flow, mask, cylinder setup, and specialist support.

Canada is a medium-priority add-on. It has a smaller population than the U.S./UK/EU, and the 2017 International Headache Society survey listed Canada as reimbursing both oxygen and device. The blocker is likely provincial/private-plan/supplier variation and doctor knowledge, rather than a single national fight. Worth a paragraph, not a full chapter, unless Canadian forum evidence shows more friction than the formal survey suggests.

Do not spend the core chapter on countries where the main problem is not "how do I get the prescription written correctly?" but "the system will not reimburse/provide home high-flow tanks for cluster headache." In those places, a prescription chapter may still explain what the script should say, but the practical help likely belongs in the welding/industrial oxygen, self-pay, or country-specific advocacy sections.

## 1. Where the bulk of diagnosable patients probably reside

### Baseline prevalence

The most useful planning assumption is that cluster headache prevalence is roughly similar across regions, with large uncertainty:

- Fischera et al. meta-analysis: lifetime prevalence 124 per 100,000, roughly 1 in 1,000; 1-year prevalence 53 per 100,000. The paper says prevalence appeared relatively stable and independent of region in the available population studies, though those studies were mostly Europe/North America.
- A 2025 diagnostic-delay meta-analysis repeats the 124/100,000 estimate and finds an overall diagnostic delay of about 10.4 years, with delay decreasing over time but still substantial.

Using 124 per 100,000 as a rough lifetime case estimate:

| Region/country | 2024 population used | Approx lifetime CH cases | Practical relevance |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| European Union | 450.2M | 558k | Large, but fragmented by country/language/reimbursement. |
| United States | 340.1M | 422k | Biggest single English-language target; access friction is high. |
| Japan | 124.0M | 154k | Large high-income pool, but non-English and reimbursement historically poor. |
| United Kingdom | 69.2M | 86k | High-impact English-language add-on; strong formal oxygen pathway. |
| South Korea | 51.8M | 64k | High-income pool; recent Korean literature notes insurance/tank access barriers. |
| Canada | 41.3M | 51k | Medium English-language add-on; provincial variation likely matters. |
| Australia + New Zealand | about 32.5M | 40k | Smaller, but high relevance for English-language guide. |

This is not the same as "diagnosed patients." Diagnostic delay and misdiagnosis mean the diagnosed share is much lower than the biologic prevalence, and diagnosed patients concentrate in places with neurologists/headache clinics, accessible primary care, and patient education.

### Practical answer

If "diagnosable" means "has realistic access to a formal diagnosis once they suspect cluster headache," the bulk is probably in:

- North America, especially the United States.
- Europe/UK, especially Western/Northern Europe.
- High-income Asia-Pacific: Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand.

If "reachable by this English guide" is included, the center of gravity shifts hard toward:

- United States.
- United Kingdom/Ireland.
- Canada.
- Australia/New Zealand.

The EU likely contains more total diagnosable patients than any single English-language country, but a prescription-helper chapter loses power there unless it is country-specific. Oxygen access is not harmonized across Europe: formal EAN guidance is shared, but reimbursement and prescription systems are national.

## 2. Main blockers to prescriptions in high-diagnosis regions

### Cross-region blockers

These repeat almost everywhere:

- Diagnosis delay: cluster headache is distinctive but still often misdiagnosed. The 2025 meta-analysis estimated mean delay at 10.43 years.
- Doctor unfamiliarity: ClusterBusters says many patients have to educate doctors, and cites U.S. survey evidence that 44% of oxygen users had to suggest oxygen to their doctors.
- Wrong script: low flow, no "as needed" wording, nasal cannula, simple mask, concentrator instead of compressed gas, no portable cylinder.
- Supplier mismatch: oxygen suppliers often think in COPD terms, asking for oxygen saturation/hypoxemia documentation or trying to supply concentrators.
- Reimbursement/coverage: IHS survey found oxygen reimbursed in only 50% of responding countries and fully reimbursed with device in only 41%.
- Logistics: too few tanks, slow refills, no portable cylinders, regulator capped at 8-15 LPM, no high-quality non-rebreather/demand-valve setup.

### United States

Main blockers:

- Prescriber uncertainty or refusal: PCPs and even some neurologists may not know oxygen is first-line or may worry about oxygen toxicity.
- Incorrect prescription: forum and ClusterBusters resource patterns repeatedly point to too-low flows, unspecified flow, concentrators, and missing non-rebreather mask language.
- Insurance denials and variability: private insurance may cover, deny, or place oxygen under deductible. Appeals and letters of medical necessity are a recurring forum theme.
- Medicare/Medicaid confusion: CMS removed the national coverage determination for home oxygen for cluster headache effective 2021, and the retired NCD page says coverage decisions are now made by Medicare Administrative Contractors. Practically, this means "not nationally excluded" is not the same as "easy and uniform."
- DME suppliers operating on COPD assumptions: one ClusterBusters forum thread warns that suppliers may ask for blood oxygen saturation testing unless the prescription clearly says cluster headache and high-flow use.
- Self-pay friction: forum users report needing to call multiple oxygen suppliers, sometimes finding self-pay cheaper or more workable than insurance, and sometimes facing suppliers who refuse or only lease equipment.

Best helper content for U.S. patients:

- Exact prescription wording.
- ICD-10 and HCPCS language as optional support, with caveat that billing details change.
- One-page doctor packet: AHS/EAN/NICE/Cohen JAMA evidence and safety note.
- Insurance script: call insurer, ask DME benefit coverage, ask whether cluster headache is covered, appeal denial, request peer-to-peer, attach guideline/trial, ask for letter of medical necessity.
- Supplier script: "compressed gas cylinders, not concentrator; non-rebreather mask/reservoir; regulator capable of at least 15 LPM, preferably 25 LPM; enough cylinders for multiple daily attacks."
- Self-pay branch: if prescription exists but coverage fails, call local medical oxygen suppliers as self-pay, ask cash refill/rental price, buy mask/regulator separately where legal and appropriate.

### United Kingdom

Formal situation:

- NICE says to offer oxygen and/or subcutaneous or nasal triptan for acute cluster headache.
- NICE specifies 100% oxygen at at least 12 L/min with non-rebreathing mask and reservoir bag, and arranging home and ambulatory oxygen.
- NHS England's HOOF Part A includes cluster headache as clinical code 18.
- OUCH(UK) tells patients in England/Wales to use a partly completed HOOF and ask the GP to complete patient/surgery details and submit it to the oxygen supplier.

Main blockers:

- GP unfamiliarity with cluster headache oxygen and HOOF.
- Need for neurologist/headache specialist diagnosis or recommendation, especially if the GP is uncertain.
- HOOF admin errors, missing consent/risk forms, or failure to request ambulatory cylinders.
- Equipment mismatch: flow, non-rebreather/reservoir, demand-valve where appropriate.
- Regional differences: England/Wales HOOF guidance should not be presented as automatically identical for Scotland/Northern Ireland.

Best helper content:

- A UK callout box rather than a whole separate chapter.
- Include exact NICE recommendation numbers, HOOF code 18, and "home and ambulatory oxygen."
- Explain that the issue is often not persuading the NHS that oxygen is valid; it is getting the right form completed correctly and urgently.

### European Union / wider Europe

Formal situation:

- EAN 2023 gives a strong recommendation for 100% oxygen at at least 12 L/min over 15 minutes for acute attacks.
- The IHS 2017 survey showed very different reimbursement status by country. Full oxygen/device reimbursement was present in some countries such as France, Germany, Netherlands, Portugal, Hungary, Canada, China, and the UK in that survey, but not in others such as Austria, Italy, Japan, Romania, Serbia, Slovakia, Turkey, and the Philippines. Switzerland reimbursed the device but not oxygen in that survey.

Main blockers:

- Country-specific reimbursement and supplier rules.
- National prescription format and physician authority: GP versus neurologist versus headache clinic.
- Mask/device reimbursement gaps.
- Outdated or low-flow prescribing.

Strategic implication:

- Do not try to write "Europe" instructions beyond the universal prescription specs and the EAN guideline.
- If expanding later, pick countries based on language/audience and current reimbursement: Germany, France, Netherlands, Italy, Spain, Nordics would each need separate verification.

### Canada

Formal situation:

- IHS 2017 survey listed Canada as reimbursing oxygen and device for cluster headache, with no age restriction.

Likely blockers:

- Province/territory differences and private-plan differences.
- Doctor unfamiliarity and incorrect prescription details.
- Supplier substitution or low-flow/regulator/mask issues.
- Patients with no coverage or slow coverage may still need a self-pay path.

Strategic implication:

- Include Canada as a shorter "same script, province-specific coverage" note unless forum/account research shows a large Canadian access fight.

### Australia / New Zealand

Formal situation:

- Headache Australia describes high-flow oxygen as first-line/cornerstone acute treatment and notes Australian prescribing guidelines often cite 7-12 L/min while many headache specialists advocate 12-15 L/min.
- Australia has a meaningful English-language population but smaller absolute case count than the U.S./UK/Canada combined.

Likely blockers:

- GP familiarity and specialist referral.
- Conservative flow prescriptions that may underperform.
- Home oxygen supplier setup: tank versus concentrator, mask, regulator, refills.
- State/territory and private-insurance variation needs further checking before drafting detailed instructions.

Strategic implication:

- A practical Australia box may be more useful than a Canada box if the guide's readers skew toward patient forums and English-language support groups.
- Before publishing detailed Australian instructions, verify current state/territory home oxygen access rules and whether cluster headache is recognized by public subsidy programs.

### Japan and South Korea

Formal situation:

- IHS 2017 survey listed Japan as not reimbursing oxygen/device for cluster headache.
- South Korea was listed as reimbursing oxygen but not device, with age restrictions, in the 2017 survey; a 2024 Korean study states oxygen tanks are not covered by insurance for CH in South Korea and that coverage mostly concerns concentrators limited to low flow.

Strategic implication:

- There are many possible patients, especially Japan, but a prescription helper in English is probably not the highest-value intervention.
- In South Korea, the blocker appears to be coverage/tank access more than just doctor wording.

## 3. Regions where a prescription helper is most likely to counterfactually help

### High counterfactual value

United States:

- Large audience.
- Prescription is legally meaningful.
- Treatment is guideline-backed.
- Access is neither automatic nor impossible.
- The known blockers are exactly what a helper can address: doctor education, script wording, insurance appeal, DME/supplier troubleshooting, self-pay medical oxygen path.

United Kingdom, especially England/Wales:

- Strong official pathway exists.
- Helper can reduce GP/HOOF/admin friction.
- The win condition is clear and concrete: correct HOOF plus correct equipment.

Australia:

- Smaller than U.S./UK but good fit for English guide.
- Likely enough friction around flow rates, prescribing norms, and home oxygen logistics for a helper to matter.
- Needs more local verification before giving detailed subsidy instructions.

### Medium counterfactual value

Canada:

- Prescription helper can prevent wrong equipment.
- Coverage may already be more favorable than the U.S., at least according to older IHS survey data.
- Needs province-specific nuance.

Selected EU countries with reimbursement but uneven execution:

- Germany, France, Netherlands, Portugal, Hungary, Nordics might benefit from translated/localized boxes.
- English generic chapter will be less counterfactual because local forms and reimbursement systems matter.

### Lower counterfactual value for a prescription chapter

Countries where reimbursement/home oxygen access is absent or highly restricted:

- Japan, Italy, Austria, Turkey, Philippines, Romania, Serbia, Slovakia, and others per the 2017 IHS survey, pending current verification.
- In these places, prescription wording alone may not unlock access. A "what the prescription should say" section is still useful, but practical help likely shifts to self-pay, industrial/welding oxygen, concentrators as a compromise, or advocacy.

Countries where oxygen is straightforward once diagnosis is made:

- The prescription chapter still helps with specs, but a long access-navigation section has lower marginal value.
- The UK is partly in this category formally, but not practically: the HOOF path is clear enough that a concise helper likely helps a lot.

## Recommended chapter strategy

### Core universal section

Lead with a universal "what the prescription must say" box:

- 100% oxygen.
- Compressed gas cylinders, not a concentrator.
- Non-rebreather mask with reservoir bag, cluster mask, or demand valve.
- At least 12-15 L/min; ask for up to 25 L/min where possible.
- "Use as needed at onset of cluster headache attack."
- 15-20 minutes per attack, repeat for multiple daily attacks as medically appropriate.
- Home cylinder plus portable/ambulatory cylinders.

### Then branch by region

1. U.S. path:
   - Doctor conversation.
   - Exact script.
   - Insurance/DME path.
   - Denial appeal.
   - Self-pay medical supplier fallback.

2. UK path:
   - NICE + HOOF + code 18.
   - GP/specialist letter.
   - Home and ambulatory oxygen.
   - Safety/risk assessment and urgent delivery.

3. Australia/Canada short boxes:
   - Same prescription specs.
   - Ask headache specialist if GP hesitates.
   - Check state/province/private-plan rules.
   - Do not accept concentrator/low-flow substitution.

4. "If your country does not reimburse home oxygen for CH":
   - The prescription may still help with self-pay medical oxygen.
   - If medical route is blocked or too slow, point to the welding oxygen chapter, with clear safety/legal disclaimers.

## Extra questions worth answering before final drafting

- What country split does the guide's actual audience have? ClusterBusters/forum audience is U.S.-heavy, but the doc may rank in search internationally.
- Does the guide want to optimize for "medical oxygen only" first, or explicitly route users to self-pay and welding alternatives early when delays are dangerous?
- Do we want downloadable doctor letters by region?
- Do we want exact U.S. ICD-10/HCPCS codes in patient-facing copy? They are useful, but billing codes change and can make the chapter feel brittle.
- Should the UK section include only England/Wales or also Scotland/Northern Ireland after verification?
- Can we get current private ClusterBusters forum evidence from the user's account, especially for 2024-2026 U.S. Medicare/MAC outcomes and Australia/Canada access?
