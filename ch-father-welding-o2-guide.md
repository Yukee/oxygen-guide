There is always discussion here about welding oxygen. It is used by at least 15 percent of people with CH, often because a doctor is too ignorant and/or lazy to prescribe medical oxygen, and sometimes because it's less expensive for them. Although there have been no reports here of anyone experiencing negative consequences from using welding O2, I am required to issue the disclaimer that it is not medically recommended and you use it at your own risk. With that said, I am just going to paste in here some relevant recent posts about welding O2. I'm sorry for doing it in this lazy way that might require you to read more than necessary, but I don't have the energy to create a whole new document. I've put in some headings that might help.

## Tanks and Sizes

You get tanks from a welding supply or "industrial gas" company. Look it up on google. You'll probably want at least two tanks/cylinders/bottles — the largest one you can handle for home use, and a smaller one, maybe 60 cu ft or even 40 cu ft, for portability. spiny recommends 90 cu ft for the home one and 60 for the portable one, and Dallas Denny has had his home tank much bigger than 90. spiny notes that the 60 is not required to be capped when traveling, so you can have your mask attached to it on the road. (Medical tank designations, such as "E" tank or "M" tank, are not used with industrial O2. Do not mention them.)

As I say, the bigger you can handle the better, since it'll keep you from having to lug it back to get it refilled. Same is true for the portable one, but in a lower range. For me personally, a 90 cu ft tank would be more than I would want to get in and out of the trunk of a car. The problem with the initial purchase for many people is that they don't know what size they can handle when they make their first purchase. That's why I recommend starting relatively small. You can always trade up in the future. But that's just my advice, and it might not be good advice.

You can roughly figure out how long a tank will last by converting cu ft to liters and dividing by a number, which I'll call 20 here, which would be the flow rate you'd be using (of course, industrial O2 regulators don't have flow rate settings as medical regulators do, so you'd just be guessing, but an effective flow rate — the rate that makes sure the bag is full each time you're ready to inhale — is typically between 15 and 25. YMMV, of course.) So, a 60 cu ft tank is about 1700 liters (you can get this info by typing into google [60 cu ft = liters]). Divided by 20 liters per minute, that means you'd get roughly 85 minutes of use from it. (I will note here again that I and some others have observed that O2 treatment for CH seems to become less effective as the tank pressure (the amount left in the tank) goes down; but I'm not sure that everyone would agree with that. The last third of the tank is almost useless for my daughter (she's the one who has CH).)

## Regulator and Mask

Ideally, you'd want a regulator and a mask for each. Note also that standard industrial regulators do not have the barbed fitting that a mask is most easily attached to. You can kluge that, or you can buy a plastic adapter for a few bucks at many hardware stores or online, or you look for a regulator that comes with an adapter with the barbed fitting. A regulator you buy at an industrial supply store will probably be considerably more expensive than one you buy online, and it might not have that barbed thingie. Recommending a specific regulator used to be simpler, because Harbor Freight had a perfect one that was inexpensive. You want one that has an adapter for a barbed fitting that will hold your mask hose most easily and effectively. CGA 540 is the type that fits all welding O2 tanks, no matter what size.

## Cart and Wrench

You probably also want a cart, too. And you'll need a large adjustable wrench for attaching the regulator.

## Maintenance and Safety

Spiny notes: Just know not to use grease, oil or any lubricant or teflon tape on the threads of the tank when you screw on your mask. You never use those around O2. All that you really need is a big adjustable crescent wrench to attach the mask to the tank. Tighten the nut on the regulator as tight as you can. Turn the regulator off with the dial. Open the valve on the tank and listen — no sound means no leakage. With the regulator off, any hiss of escaping gas would be coming from the nut you just screwed on. Of course, don't smoke while you're using O2, and follow other safety precautions the same as you would for medical O2 use.

## Buying

You just buy a tank, or a couple of tanks, and do not mention that they're for medical use (and do not use medical tank designations, such as "E" tank or "M" tank). 95 percent of the time, you're not going to be asked any questions. Acetylene might be mentioned. You don't need it. If you don't feel comfortable with the buying process because you don't know what questions you might be asked, it seems that a lot of people make up some cover story in case they're asked, like their nephew is coming to town and going to do some welding and asked you to pick up these tanks for him. You don't know nothin' beyond that. Getting them "refilled" (actually, they're usually going to swap your returned tank for a different one that's already filled) should be no issue, at least if you're returning it to a branch of the company you got it from. Again, the sales person might chat with you about something, but you can play dumb.
