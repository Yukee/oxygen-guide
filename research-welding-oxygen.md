# Research Notes: Welding Oxygen for Cluster Headache Therapy

*Internal research document — not patient-facing. Last updated April 2026.*

## Why Patients Turn to Welding Oxygen

The Rozen 2010 US Cluster Headache Survey (the largest US survey of CH patients at the time) found that 16% of patients considered medical oxygen unaffordable, and **12% were purchasing welding-grade oxygen as a cheaper alternative**. A separate AHS/AMF joint statement noted that 41% of patients prescribed home oxygen were denied coverage by their insurers. The combination of insurance barriers, prescription difficulties, and cost pressure pushes a meaningful minority of patients toward industrial sources.

ClusterBusters forums contain multiple long-running threads where members discuss sourcing, setting up, and using welding oxygen — it is an established practice in the community, not a fringe idea.


## The Gas Itself: Is It Actually Different?

### The short answer: chemically, often not

Both medical and welding oxygen are produced by the same industrial process (typically cryogenic fractional distillation of air). In many facilities the gas comes from the **same bulk tank** — the difference is in downstream handling, testing, and paperwork.

### Purity specifications

| Grade | Minimum purity | Key spec |
|---|---|---|
| USP Medical Oxygen | ≥ 99.0% O₂ | FDA-regulated as a drug; moisture ≤ 67 ppm |
| Industrial / Welding | ≥ 99.2–99.5% O₂ (typical) | No FDA drug oversight; no mandatory moisture limit |
| Aviation Breathing (ABO) | ≥ 99.5% O₂ | Tight moisture control; also breathing-grade |

The irony: welding oxygen often *exceeds* 99.5% purity in practice because modern air-separation plants don't have a separate "dirty" production line. The gas itself is essentially identical.

### So where does the risk come from?

**Not the gas — the cylinder and the handling.** This is the critical distinction.

1. **Medical cylinders** must be evacuated, cleaned, and inspected before every refill under FDA Current Good Manufacturing Practice (CGMP) rules. They have full lot traceability and are dedicated to oxygen.

2. **Welding cylinders** are not subject to these rules. The main risks:
   - **Previous contents.** A cylinder that previously held acetylene, argon, or another gas could retain traces. Standard practice is to vacuum-purge before filling, but this is done to prevent explosions, not to protect a person breathing from it.
   - **Moisture and rust.** Without mandated evacuation and cleaning between fills, internal corrosion can develop over time. Iron oxide particles could theoretically be inhaled.
   - **Compressor oil.** Though modern compression largely uses oil-free methods, older or poorly maintained equipment could introduce hydrocarbon traces.
   - **No certificate of analysis.** With medical oxygen you get a paper trail confirming the tested purity of each batch. With welding oxygen you get none.

### What does this actually mean in practice?

The aviation community had this same debate years ago. Pilot and journalist John Deakin investigated in his well-known *Pelican's Perch* column (AVweb) and concluded that the gas from the same supplier is typically identical regardless of label — the old distinctions trace back to decades-old compression methods that are no longer in use. Scuba divers have reached similar conclusions.

However: the *cylinder* matters. A brand-new cylinder filled from a reputable gas supplier is a different risk profile from an old, well-used cylinder with unknown fill history.


## Cost Comparison

### Welding oxygen (self-pay, no prescription)

- **Cylinder rental:** ~$18/month for a 60 cu ft tank at Airgas (a major US supplier)
- **Refills:** ~$20–40 per fill depending on cylinder size
- **Regulator:** A welding regulator shows PSI, not liters per minute — usable but imprecise for therapy. A medical-style 0–25 LPM regulator for CGA 540 (large cylinder) or CGA 870 (small cylinder) connections can be purchased online for $30–80.
- **Mask:** Non-rebreather mask or demand valve must be purchased separately ($5–30 for NRB, $50+ for demand valve)
- **No prescription required** — welding supply shops sell to anyone

### Medical oxygen (with prescription)

- **With insurance:** Copay varies; many plans cover it but the 41% denial rate cited above is a major barrier
- **Without insurance / self-pay through DME:** Often $200–400+/month for delivery and cylinder rental
- **Self-pay through a gas supplier (e.g., Airgas medical division):** Closer to welding prices, but requires a valid prescription and a physician willing to write one

### Bottom line on cost

The gas itself costs roughly the same from the same supplier. The cost difference comes from the medical-supply delivery model (DME companies, insurance billing, rental markups). Patients who self-pay through an industrial gas supplier with a prescription often pay comparable prices to the welding route.


## Legal Situation (Brief)

- In the US, medical oxygen is FDA-classified as a prescription drug. Purchasing it requires a valid Rx.
- Welding oxygen is an industrial commodity — **no prescription is needed** to buy it.
- There is no law against *breathing* welding oxygen. The legal barrier is on the supply side: a gas supplier cannot *sell you medical oxygen* without a prescription, but they can sell you welding oxygen without one.
- Patients face **no criminal legal risk** for using welding oxygen on themselves. The risk is civil/liability: if something went wrong, the gas supplier's liability would be limited because they sold it for industrial use.
- Physicians cannot "prescribe" welding oxygen. If a provider learns a patient is using it, they are likely to advise against it for liability reasons even if they privately consider the risk minimal.


## What the Patient Community Actually Does

Based on ClusterBusters forum discussions and the Rozen survey data:

1. **Many patients use welding oxygen successfully for years.** Forum reports describe identical efficacy to medical-grade O₂ for aborting attacks.
2. **The typical setup:** Large cylinder (60–120 cu ft) from Airgas or a local welding supply shop, with either a welding regulator (adjusted by feel) or a purchased medical-style LPM regulator.
3. **ClusterBusters officially recommends prescribed medical oxygen only**, and does not endorse welding oxygen. Their public position is that welding oxygen "could be potentially dangerous."
4. **Patients who use it tend to frame it as a harm-reduction choice:** they weigh the theoretical (and small) risk of cylinder contaminants against the very real harm of untreated cluster attacks.


## Risk Assessment

### Realistic risks (small but non-zero)

- Trace contaminants from a poorly maintained or previously contaminated cylinder
- Lack of quality assurance — you're trusting the supplier's general practices rather than a certified batch
- No recourse if something goes wrong

### Theoretical risks (often overstated)

- The gas purity argument is largely moot — the same gas comes from the same plant
- Claims about "chemical pneumonitis" from welding oxygen appear in safety literature but are based on worst-case industrial accident scenarios, not on the reality of breathing from a modern, clean oxygen cylinder

### Factors that actually affect safety

- **Age and condition of the cylinder** — newer is better
- **Supplier reputation** — a major supplier like Airgas vs. a random shop matters
- **Whether the cylinder is oxygen-dedicated** — a cylinder that has only ever held oxygen is vastly safer than one with mixed-gas history
- **Regulator setup** — using a proper medical-style LPM regulator is safer than guessing with a welding regulator


## Open Questions / Needs Further Investigation

1. Are there any documented cases of adverse events from breathing welding oxygen? (I found none in my research, but absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence — adverse events would likely go unreported.)
2. What do gas suppliers like Airgas actually do with welding cylinders between fills? Interviews or FOIA-style inquiries could clarify whether vacuum purging is standard.
3. How does the picture differ outside the US? Some countries may have more or less distinction between industrial and medical grades.
4. Could the guide recommend a middle path — e.g., self-paying for medical oxygen through a gas supplier, which avoids both the DME markup *and* the cylinder-handling concerns?

## What a Naive Patient Needs to Know: The Full Welding O₂ Setup

*Compiled from ClusterBusters forums, Cluster Headache Warriors guides, and welding community discussions.*

### Step 1: Find a supplier

The most commonly mentioned supplier in patient forums is **Airgas**, which has locations across the US. Other options include local welding supply shops, farm supply stores, and industrial gas distributors.

**What to expect at the store:**

- You're buying a commodity — oxygen for welding. No prescription, no medical questions. You do not need to explain why you want it, and the staff won't ask.
- You'll need to either **rent** a cylinder or **buy one outright**. Some locations require opening a basic customer account (name, address, credit card); others serve walk-ins.
- At some Airgas locations, cylinders over 40 cu ft require a commercial account with a tax ID. Policies vary by branch — call ahead.
- You can also buy used cylinders on Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace, or similar platforms and bring them in for exchange/refill. If you do, you must be able to prove ownership (a bill of sale is sufficient).

**Rent vs. buy:**

- **Renting** is simpler: ~$18/month for a 60 cu ft tank at Airgas. You swap the empty for a full one; the supplier maintains the cylinder.
- **Buying** avoids ongoing rental fees, but you own the cylinder and are responsible for its hydrostatic testing (required every 5–10 years depending on cylinder type). Purchased cylinders are filled on-site rather than exchanged.

### Step 2: Choose a cylinder size

Welding cylinders are sold in cubic feet (cf), not the letter designations (E, M, H) used in medical oxygen. Here's how the sizes map and how long they last at high flow:

| Welding size | Approx. cubic feet | Approx. liters | Duration at 15 LPM | Duration at 25 LPM | Connection |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Small (R) | 20 cf | ~566 L | ~38 min | ~23 min | CGA 540 |
| Medium | 40 cf | ~1,133 L | ~75 min | ~45 min | CGA 540 |
| Standard | 60 cf | ~1,699 L | ~113 min | ~68 min | CGA 540 |
| Large | 80 cf | ~2,265 L | ~151 min | ~91 min | CGA 540 |
| Extra large | 125 cf | ~3,540 L | ~236 min | ~142 min | CGA 540 |

**Key point:** All standard welding oxygen cylinders use the **CGA 540** valve connection — a large threaded post. This is different from the CGA 870 pin-index connection used on small medical E-tanks. Your regulator must match your cylinder's valve.

**Practical advice from the forums:** Most patients keep one large cylinder at home (80–125 cf) and, if budget allows, a smaller portable one. A 60 cf tank gives you roughly 4–7 aborts at 15 LPM (assuming 15 minutes each) — fine for occasional attacks, but heavy cluster periods can burn through it fast.

### Step 3: Get a regulator

This is the part where most newcomers trip up. A welding regulator shows output pressure in **PSI**, not liters per minute — it's designed for cutting torches, not breathing. You *can* use one (crack the valve until the reservoir bag fills quickly), but you're guessing at flow rate.

**Better option:** Buy a medical-style oxygen regulator with a CGA 540 fitting and a **liters-per-minute (LPM) flowmeter**. These are widely available online (Amazon, medical supply sites, specialty vendors) for $30–80.

What to look for:

- **CGA 540 inlet** (matches welding cylinders)
- **0–25 LPM flow range** — many cheap regulators max out at 15 LPM, which the community considers the bare minimum. 25 LPM is strongly preferred.
- **DISS or barb outlet** — DISS (Diameter Index Safety System) is a threaded medical fitting; a barbed outlet accepts standard oxygen tubing directly. Either works.

**Specific products mentioned in forums:**

- The "Responsive Respiratory" 25 LPM CGA 540 regulator (available online, ~$50–70)
- Various 0–25 LPM EMS-grade regulators on Amazon
- Avoid: regulators labeled "0–8 LPM" or "pediatric" — these are too low for cluster headache therapy

### Step 4: Get a mask

The standard hospital non-rebreather mask (NRB) works, but the cluster headache community is emphatic that better options exist:

- **O2ptimask** (~$25 from clusterheadaches.com): Designed specifically for cluster headache patients. No side vents, tight seal, available in two sizes. Also has a mouth-tube option. This is the most commonly recommended mask in the forums.
- **Standard non-rebreather mask** (~$5–10, widely available): Functional but leaky. Side vents let room air in and dilute the O₂ concentration. Some patients tape over the vents.
- **Demand valve** (~$300+): Delivers 100% O₂ on demand as fast as you can inhale — no wasted gas, no waiting for the bag to refill. Superior performance but expensive. Uses O₂ much more efficiently, so cylinders last longer.

**Connecting mask to regulator:** You'll need standard oxygen tubing (typically 7-foot length, widely available) running from the regulator's outlet barb to the mask inlet. This is the same green tubing used in medical settings.

### Step 5: Assemble and test

1. **Secure the cylinder** upright — chain or strap it to a wall, furniture, or a cylinder cart. A full large cylinder is heavy (60–130 lbs) and a fall can shear off the valve, turning it into a projectile.
2. **Attach the regulator** to the CGA 540 valve by hand-tightening, then snugging with a wrench. The connection should be metal-to-metal; do not use Teflon tape or any lubricant (oils + high-pressure oxygen = fire risk).
3. **Open the cylinder valve slowly** — a quarter turn to start. Check for leaks by listening and feeling around the connection. If you hear hissing, close the valve and reseat the regulator.
4. **Connect the tubing** from regulator outlet to mask.
5. **Set flow rate** (if using LPM regulator) or adjust the valve until the reservoir bag fills quickly (if using a welding regulator).
6. **Test:** Hold the mask to your face and breathe. The bag should not fully collapse on inhalation — if it does, increase the flow rate.

### Step 6: Storage and safety at home

- **Keep away from open flame, heat sources, and grease/oil.** Oxygen doesn't burn, but it makes everything else burn faster and hotter. No smoking near the cylinder.
- **Store upright, secured.** A cylinder cart (~$30–60) is ideal.
- **Close the valve after each use.** Don't leave the cylinder open with the regulator controlling flow — if a fitting fails, you lose the entire tank (and create a fire-enriched atmosphere).
- **Ventilate.** Don't use in a tiny sealed closet. A normal room is fine.
- **Never return a completely empty cylinder.** Leave a small amount of positive pressure (50+ PSI) to prevent contaminants or moisture from entering the cylinder. The regulator gauge will show this.
- **Keep the valve cap on during transport.** This protects the valve from damage.

### Step 7: Getting refills

- **Exchange model (rented cylinders):** Bring the empty to the supplier, swap for a full one. Takes minutes.
- **Refill model (owned cylinders):** Drop off, they fill it (sometimes while you wait, sometimes next day). Cost: ~$20–40 depending on size.
- **Ask the supplier to purge/vacuum the tank before filling.** This is the single most useful risk-reduction step. Cluster Headache Warriors explicitly recommends this. Many suppliers do it routinely; asking ensures it happens.

### What the Community Organizations Say

- **ClusterBusters:** "We DO NOT recommend using anything other than prescribed medical oxygen provided by an approved vendor." They acknowledge the practice exists but won't endorse it.
- **Cluster Headache Warriors (clusterheadachewarriors.org):** Frames it as "a last resort" but provides a practical guide. Notes that the gas is the same and the real difference is cylinder chain-of-custody. Recommends asking suppliers to purge tanks before filling.
- **Clusterheadaches.com:** Sells the ClusterO2 Kit (mask + fittings) and their forums discuss welding oxygen openly.

### Things That Catch People Off Guard

Based on forum posts from first-timers:

1. **The cylinder is heavy.** An 80 cf tank weighs ~55 lbs full. Don't underestimate this if you're carrying it up stairs.
2. **You burn through gas faster than you expect.** At 25 LPM, a 60 cf tank gives you barely an hour of total use. Heavy cluster periods may need multiple refills per week.
3. **A welding regulator is confusing if you've never used one.** The gauges show tank pressure and delivery pressure in PSI, not LPM. Spending $50 on a medical-style LPM regulator is worth it.
4. **The mask matters more than you think.** A leaky mask wastes oxygen and reduces effectiveness. The O2ptimask or a well-sealed NRB with vents taped shut is a meaningful upgrade.
5. **Middle-of-the-night attacks mean fumbling with equipment in the dark while in severe pain.** Set everything up *before* a cycle starts. Practice the routine. Keep tubing connected and the mask within arm's reach of your bed.
6. **Some suppliers are friendlier than others.** If one location gives you trouble, try another. Farm supply stores and small independent welding shops are often more flexible than corporate branches.
