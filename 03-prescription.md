# Getting a prescription

Getting oxygen has two halves. First you have to get your doctor to write the right prescription. Then you have to get a supplier to actually deliver the right equipment. Either half can stall for months. The fight depends on where you live, so this chapter is split by country.

## The prescription itself

The single most useful thing in this chapter is the wording below. Print it. Bring it to the appointment. Ask your doctor to write it on the prescription, word for word.

> **Oxygen therapy for cluster headache. Up to 25 minutes per attack, at 15 L/min minimum, via non-rebreathing mask with reservoir bag. Both home and ambulatory provision.**

Each piece of that sentence matters:

- **15 L/min minimum.** Standard oxygen prescriptions for lung disease run at 2–6 L/min. That flow rate will not abort a cluster attack. Cluster headache needs high flow — 15 L/min is the floor in the US, 12 L/min in the UK, and many people end up wanting 25 L/min once they try it. If the regulator delivered to your door doesn't reach at least 15 L/min, it's the wrong regulator.
- **Non-rebreathing mask with reservoir bag.** A regular oxygen mask, or a nasal cannula, will not work. You need the kind of mask that has a soft plastic bag hanging off the bottom — that's the reservoir. Without it, you breathe in room air mixed with the oxygen and the treatment fails.
- **Both home and ambulatory.** "Ambulatory" is the word the prescription form uses. It just means a smaller, portable cylinder you can carry. Attacks happen at work, in restaurants, at 3 a.m. in a hotel, in the car on the way home. A single tank parked in your living room is not enough.
- **Up to 25 minutes per attack.** Most attacks abort in 10–20 minutes of breathing oxygen at high flow. The 25-minute upper limit gives you headroom and tells the supplier roughly how much gas you'll go through.

Beyond the prescription itself, ask your doctor to write a brief note in your chart confirming the diagnosis (cluster headache), describing the severity of your attacks, and stating that oxygen is medically necessary because attacks come on fast and need fast treatment. If insurance pushes back later, that note is the basis of the appeal.

Don't leave the appointment without this exact language on the script. If your doctor balks, the next sections explain what to say.

## United States

The US is the hardest country in this guide. Two distinct things can go wrong, and the fix is different for each:

1. **Your doctor writes the wrong prescription**, or refuses to write one at all.
2. **The supplier refuses to fill the prescription**, even when it's correct, even when you offer to pay cash.

Both happen. Both are routine. Neither is the end of the road.

### Step by step

1. Get the script from your doctor using the wording above.
2. **Before** you submit it anywhere, call two or three local oxygen suppliers and ask: "Will you fill an oxygen prescription with cluster headache as the diagnosis?" Some say yes. Some say no as a corporate policy. You want to know which is which before you've handed over a piece of paper.
3. If a supplier says yes: schedule the delivery, then run the [verification checklist](#verifying-what-was-delivered) the moment the driver arrives.
4. If every supplier says no: see ["If your supplier refuses"](#if-your-supplier-refuses) below.

### Insurance scenarios

#### Private insurance

Private insurers often deny oxygen for cluster headache on the first try. The denial is usually procedural, not final. Ask your prescriber to:

- Request a **doctor-to-doctor call with the insurer.** The formal name for this is a "peer-to-peer review" — a short phone call where your doctor explains the case directly to the insurer's medical reviewer. This is where most denials get reversed.
- If standard treatments haven't worked for you, ask your doctor to use the word **intractable** on the prescription and in any supporting letter. It's a clinical term that insurance reviewers respond to — it tells them the case is not routine and that more aggressive treatment is appropriate.
- Get any denial **in writing**, with the specific reason for refusal. A vague phone refusal is hard to appeal. A written denial gives you something concrete to push back against.

#### Medicare and Medicare Advantage

There is a widespread belief that Medicare doesn't cover oxygen for cluster headache. **That belief is out of date.** Medicare removed the national exclusion in September 2021. Coverage is now decided regionally, by the contractor that handles Medicare claims in your state. Many regions are now paying.

The catch: not every supplier knows this yet. Some still tell patients "Medicare doesn't cover it" out of habit. The fix is to make them actually run the claim.

> **If a US supplier or doctor says Medicare doesn't cover oxygen for cluster headache:**
> Effective 27 September 2021, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services removed the national policy that excluded oxygen for cluster headache. Coverage determinations are now made by the regional Medicare Administrative Contractor under section 1862(a)(1)(A) of the Social Security Act, as described in NCD 240.2 (Home Use of Oxygen), Section D. Ask the supplier to **submit the claim** and **provide any denial in writing** rather than refusing on the assumption it won't be covered. References: [CMS Decision Memo CAG-00296R2](https://www.cms.gov/medicare-coverage-database/view/ncacal-decision-memo.aspx?proposed=N&NCAId=301), [NCD 240.2](https://www.cms.gov/medicare-coverage-database/view/ncd.aspx?NCDId=169).

Medicare Advantage plans must cover what Original Medicare covers, but they can add their own paperwork (prior authorization, in-network supplier requirements). If you're on a Medicare Advantage plan, call the plan directly and ask: is oxygen for cluster headache covered, what prior authorization do you need, and which suppliers in my area are in-network for this?

#### Self-pay and HSA

Sometimes the insurance route is more friction than it's worth and you'd rather pay cash. This is reasonable. Tank refills can run around $140/month at the right supplier. The trick is finding the right supplier — corporate chains often refuse cash-pay for cluster headache scripts as a blanket policy, while smaller local suppliers will quietly fill them.

If a chain refuses and you know there's a willing branch elsewhere, ask to speak to a district or regional manager. The corporate "no" sometimes turns into a local "yes" once a sympathetic branch manager is involved. Keep that manager's direct number.

> **Caveat: Medicaid and VA**
> Both Medicaid and the VA vary too much to cover here. Medicaid is state-by-state with its own rules layered on top of Medicare's. VA care has its own formulary and supply chain (including community care and CHAMPVA). If you're on either: call your plan administrator directly, ask whether oxygen for cluster headache is covered, and request any denial in writing. Don't accept a phone refusal as final.

### If your supplier refuses

You called ahead and they said yes, but now they're stalling. Or they said no on the phone and you want to push back. Here are the steps, in order:

1. **Ask them why, in writing.** Email, letter, or a portal message. A written refusal is appealable. A phone refusal is not.
2. **Insist they actually run the claim.** Sometimes "we don't cover it" means the supplier never submitted anything. Ask them to submit and produce the denial from the insurer or the Medicare contractor.
3. **Ask to speak to a manager, then their manager.** District manager, then regional manager. The patient who gets oxygen is often the one who keeps asking up the chain until someone with authority steps in.
4. **Switch supplier.** A different branch of the same chain, or a different chain entirely, may have a different answer. The next call is free.
5. **Call your prescriber.** Ask whether the diagnosis code on the script can be adjusted, or whether they'd add a supporting letter emphasizing why oxygen is medically necessary in your case.

A note on what is actually happening here: some large home-medical-equipment chains have an internal company rule against filling cluster headache oxygen prescriptions, even when the patient is paying cash. That rule is the company's own — it is not Medicare's, not your insurer's, and not the law. Not all suppliers do it. The fix is to find one that doesn't.

### When to consider welding oxygen

If you've spent weeks calling suppliers and you're still not getting tanks, or if the monthly cash-pay rate is more than you can carry, the welding-oxygen route exists. It's not for everyone, and it has its own setup and safety considerations, but it is a real option used by many cluster headache patients in the US. See the [welding oxygen chapter](08-welding-oxygen.md).

### Verifying what was delivered

The supplier said yes. The driver is at your door. Before they leave, run this checklist. If anything is wrong, sort it out **now**, while the truck is still in your driveway.

- **Tank size.** You want **M-size** tanks (sometimes called M60 or M122) for home use — large enough to last a stretch of attacks. Some suppliers default to **E-size** tanks, which are the small green ones used for ambulance transport. An E tank lasts a few attacks at high flow and then it's empty. If you only got E tanks, ask for at least one M tank for home use, and keep the E tanks as your portable supply for leaving the house.
- **Regulator.** Look at the dial. Does it actually go to **15 L/min** or higher? Many standard regulators max out at 6 or 8 L/min — those are for lung-disease patients and they are useless for cluster headache. If the regulator stops at 8, ask for a **high-flow regulator** that reaches at least 15 L/min, ideally 25 L/min.
- **Mask.** Does it have a clear plastic bag hanging from the bottom? That's the reservoir. If not, it's the wrong mask. A simple mask, a Venturi mask, or a nasal cannula will not abort an attack at any flow rate. Ask for a **non-rebreathing mask with reservoir bag**.
- **Quantity.** How many tanks did they leave? Will they refill on demand, or is there a schedule? How do you call for a refill? Get this in writing or, at minimum, a phone number on the fridge.

If any of those answers is wrong and the supplier won't fix it on the spot, point to the prescription wording, ask for the right item, and if they refuse go back to the steps in [If your supplier refuses](#if-your-supplier-refuses). If you decide later you want to upgrade the mask or regulator yourself — better seal, demand valve, higher flow — see the [equipment chapter](05-equipment.md).

## United Kingdom

The UK is structurally easier than the US. Oxygen and the equipment are reimbursed by the NHS. The friction is different: the real fight is getting your GP to take cluster headache seriously and to write a prescription that meets the national guideline. Once the prescription exists, delivery and refills work.

### Step by step

1. **Get your GP to take it seriously.** Cluster headache is rare enough that many GPs have never seen a case. Bring the NICE guideline reference (in the box below) and the OUCH UK pre-filled order form (next step). You are not asking for an experimental treatment — you are asking for a NICE-recommended one.
2. **Hand the GP a HOOF pre-filled with code 18.** The HOOF — Home Oxygen Order Form — is the form your GP fills in to order home oxygen. Part A is the order itself. On that form, Primary Clinical Code **18** is the tick-box for cluster headache. You don't have to invent anything: OUCH UK provides a downloadable HOOF that's already partly filled in. Print it, bring it, hand it over. The GP signs it and sends it on.
3. **Sign the safety and consent forms.** Two short forms come with the HOOF: a safety form and a consent form. NHS paperwork calls them IHORM (Initial Home Oxygen Risk Mitigation) and HOC (Home Oxygen Consent). They're routine — not obstacles — but they have to be signed before the supplier installs anything.
4. **Confirm both a home cylinder and a portable cylinder are on the order.** A single cylinder parked in the living room isn't enough on its own. The portable cylinder — the form calls it "ambulatory" — is what lets you treat attacks away from the house.
5. **Demand valve — only if needed.** This is an upgrade, not the entry point. See [Demand valve as an upgrade](#demand-valve-as-an-upgrade) below.

> **If your GP is uncertain about prescribing oxygen for cluster headache:**
> The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) clinical guideline CG150 recommends 100% oxygen at **at least 12 L/min** via a non-rebreathing mask with reservoir bag, for up to 20 minutes per attack, and instructs the prescriber to arrange both **home and ambulatory** provision. The HOOF Part A form has a dedicated tick-box (Primary Clinical Code 18) for cluster headache — there is no need to invent paperwork. References: [NICE CG150](https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/cg150), [NHS England HOOF Part A](https://www.england.nhs.uk/coronavirus/wp-content/uploads/sites/52/2020/04/Home-Oxygen-Order-Form-HOOF-Part-A.pdf).

### Demand valve as an upgrade

A demand valve is a different kind of mask system that delivers oxygen only when you breathe in, on demand, at very high flow. Many cluster headache patients find it aborts attacks faster than a standard non-rebreathing mask, and it wastes less gas. It is a real upgrade, not a gimmick.

The catch in the UK is that it's positioned as a second-line option, not the default:

- It is approved for NHS prescription in England and Wales. It is increasingly available in Scotland.
- Some local NHS pathways restrict NHS-funded demand valves to patients with **chronic** cluster headache (attacks that don't go into remission), and offer only a non-rebreathing mask to episodic patients.
- Even where it's approved, suppliers are not always obliged to provide one — availability is patchy by region.

What this means in practice: start the standard NHS process and get the basic kit installed. If the standard mask doesn't abort attacks well enough, ask your GP or specialist to request a demand valve, citing your local pathway. If that's denied or stalls, a common shortcut is to **buy a demand valve privately** (around £200) and use it with the cylinders the NHS already supplies. See the [OUCH UK demand-valve information leaflet](https://ouchuk.org/sites/default/files/downloads/high_flow_oxygen_therapy_2022_v8_vh.pdf) for the details.

## Canada

In Canada, the most common blocker is that your GP refers you to a neurologist instead of writing the prescription themselves — and neurologist waitlists in some provinces run a year or more. Provincial home-oxygen coverage exists, but it's set up around lung disease, not headache.

What helps:

- **Ask your GP to write it directly.** The wording in Section 1 is something a GP can sign. There is no rule that an oxygen prescription for cluster headache has to come from a neurologist. Bring the wording, explain that this oxygen is used in short bursts during attacks, and ask plainly. Some GPs will sign once they understand the request.
- **If a specialist really is required**, ask about virtual consultations or referrals to another province — those waits can be months shorter than in-person ones in your home province.
- **If you can't ride out the wait**, welding oxygen is reportedly easier to source in Canada than in many other countries. See the [welding oxygen chapter](08-welding-oxygen.md).

## Australia and New Zealand

In Australia and New Zealand, oxygen for cluster headache usually has to come from a neurologist, and the out-of-pocket cost — for the visits and for the oxygen itself — is much higher than in the UK or in many US insurance plans. The wall here is mostly money, not paperwork.

A GP referral to a neurologist is the usual starting point. Expect at least a month's wait, and a long drive if you're rural. Because welding-grade oxygen is chemically identical to medical-grade and far cheaper to refill, many patients in this region end up on the [welding oxygen route](08-welding-oxygen.md) instead of the prescription one.

## Continental Europe

Continental Europe varies. In **Germany, France, and Scandinavia**, oxygen and the equipment are usually reimbursed and the prescription itself is rarely the blocker — your fight, if you have one, is with the equipment delivered (a regulator that only goes to 15 L/min, no demand valve), and the [equipment chapter](05-equipment.md) is the right next step. In **Belgium and the Netherlands**, oxygen is generally available but the demand-valve fight resembles the UK's. In **Eastern Europe**, reimbursement and infrastructure are the binding constraints and what's in this chapter won't fully solve them — local patient organizations are the better starting point.

## If you've been blocked for months

It is depressingly common to spend a year or more trying to get oxygen, and to fail. If that's you, you are not alone, and you are not doing anything wrong.

Two doors exist outside the prescription system:

- **[Welding oxygen](08-welding-oxygen.md)** — chemically identical to medical oxygen, sold over the counter at welding suppliers in most countries, with its own setup, safety, and equipment considerations.
- **Buying privately.** A demand valve (around £200 in the UK), a non-rebreathing mask (under £20 anywhere), and a high-flow regulator (~£100) can be ordered online without a prescription. The cylinders are the part you can't buy on a card — and that's where the welding-oxygen route fills the gap.

Neither of these is a recommendation. Both are simply the doors that exist when the prescription door has stayed shut for too long.
