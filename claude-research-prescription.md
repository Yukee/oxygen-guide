# Research notes — prescription chapter strategy

Research date: 2026-05-07. Sources: Clusterbusters forum threads (logged in as Nicoco) + epidemiology literature.

## TL;DR

- **Audience that's reachable in English**: ~70-80% US + UK + Canada + Australia/NZ + English-reading Western Europeans. US dwarfs everyone else by raw volume.
- **Blocker patterns are sharply country-specific** and almost identical within each country.
- **80/20 recommendation**: Primary focus US; secondary focus UK; light touch on Canada/Australia; one paragraph for continental EU.

---

## 1. Where do diagnosable patients live?

Biological prevalence ≈ 0.1% globally. Country-level *measured* prevalence ranges 0 (Greece) → 381/100k (Norway) — that variance is diagnostic infrastructure, not biology.

| Country | Population | Potential cases (0.1%) | Forum presence |
|---|---|---|---|
| US | ~330M | ~330k | Dominant |
| Germany | ~84M | ~84k | Low (English barrier) |
| UK | ~68M | ~68k | Strong |
| France | ~68M | ~68k | Low (English barrier) |
| Italy | ~59M | ~59k | Low (English barrier) |
| Canada | ~40M | ~40k | Strong |
| Australia/NZ | ~30M | ~30k | Moderate |

Sources:
- [Fischera et al., 2008](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1111/j.1468-2982.2008.01592.x) — meta-analysis of CH prevalence

- [Schor et al., 2021](https://headachejournal.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/head.14237) — diagnostic delay; only 15.2% of pediatric-onset patients diagnosed before 18

## 2. Per-region blocker patterns

### United States — biggest pool, hard mode

Forum thread: [Newbie - Issues Getting Oxygen Prescription Filled](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/29481-newbie-issues-getting-oxygen-prescription-filled/) — Mordecai (Virginia, April 2026)

**Pattern**: doctor writes prescription → supplier refuses to fill citing CH-not-covered, even with cash.

> "the medical supply store calls and tells me insurance won't cover it since it's for CH and they don't accept out of pocket payments??? WTF! I called the only other medical supply store and they asked what the prescription was and told me the same thing insurance won't cover so I can fill your prescription." — Mordecai

> "the area I'm in only has two suppliers and the one manager I've spoken to this afternoon tells me we don't supply oxygen to cluster headache patients…I ask why?!? … and get the same response the next 5 times" — Mordecai

**Resolution pattern**: climb the corporate ladder until a sympathetic local branch manager intervenes:

> "She said she received the emails from the corporate managers giving me a hard time about insurance not covering it … 'you put my # in your phone and deal with me only!'" — Mordecai

**Equipment substitution problem** ([CHfather's reply](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/29481-newbie-issues-getting-oxygen-prescription-filled/?do=findComment&comment=114898)):

> "A prescription should read something like 'Oxygen therapy for cluster headache: Up to 25 minutes of 15 liters/minute with nonrebreather mask.' … You will probably want to switch out the mask and regulator at some point."

Same VA-area pattern, second patient ([Clairmon](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/29481-newbie-issues-getting-oxygen-prescription-filled/), May 2026):

> "My doctor is willing to prescribe Oxygen but not experienced, I'm his first CH patient, and he doesn't know how to work with an oxygen supplier. … Adding insurance paperwork and hoopjumps to something you are highly motivated to get done quickly never seems smart."

**Sub-blockers**:
- Doctor doesn't know what to write (flow rate, mask type, tank size)
- **Medicare is no longer nationally excluded.** CMS removed NCD 240.2.2 effective 2021-09-27 and modified NCD 240.2 so home oxygen for cluster headache became MAC-discretionary ([CMS decision memo CAG-00296R2](https://www.cms.gov/medicare-coverage-database/view/ncacal-decision-memo.aspx?proposed=N&NCAId=301), [retired NCD 240.2.2](https://www.cms.gov/medicare-coverage-database/view/ncd.aspx?ncdid=343&ncdver=3), [current NCD 240.2](https://www.cms.gov/medicare-coverage-database/view/ncd.aspx?NCDId=169)). Implementation transmittals slipped (originally 2022-06-14, extended to 2023-01-03 per [HME News](https://www.hmenews.com/article/cms-changes-date-for-oxygen-ncd-implementation)) — patient-facing copy should ignore that and just say "removed effective September 27, 2021; coverage is now MAC-discretionary."
- **Coverage vs. access are two different fights.** *Coverage* is MAC-dependent: the regulatory wedge is to cite [NCD 240.2 §D](https://www.cms.gov/medicare-coverage-database/view/ncd.aspx?NCDId=169) referencing SSA §1862(a)(1)(A), and the chapter should hand readers that exact phrase. *Practical access* is supplier-dependent: even where the MAC will pay, the supplier may not run the claim, may refuse CH-coded scripts as a corporate policy, or may only deliver the wrong equipment. The advice differs — for MAC fights, regulatory citations; for supplier fights, "make them run the claim and produce the denial in writing," ladder-climb to a local manager, or switch supplier. Forum evidence (2022–2025) confirms the "available in principle, fight in practice" pattern:
  - [CHfather, Dec 2025](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/28518-intractable-chronic-cluster-headaches/) — concise summary the chapter can paraphrase: *"It was 9/27/2021 that the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services removed the national policy that O2 was not covered for CH. They left it up to regional offices to decide. I think that is still the status, and I think most regional offices are covering it."*
  - [CHfather, Jan 2024](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/6999-insurance-coverage-for-oxygen/) — quotes the operative regulatory language readers can wave at suppliers: *"[C]overage determinations will be made by the Medicare Administrative Contractors under section 1862(a)(1)(A) of the Social Security Act, as allowed and described in Chapter 1, Section 240.2 (Home Use of Oxygen), Subsection D, of Publication 100-03 of the NCD Manual."*
  - [eagleswings (Indiana), Jan 2024](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/6999-insurance-coverage-for-oxygen/) — concrete success + the "make them actually run the claim" tactic: *"I have my O2 approved by my Medicare plan. My O2 company refused to run it at first, I told them to humor me and they called within 24 hours saying it was covered (they were shocked...I was not)... ALSO make sure the O2 company runs it — ask them to show the denial."*
  - [spiny (NC), Jan 2024](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/6999-insurance-coverage-for-oxygen/) — Lincare-specific outcome: *"I'm in NC and it was covered last year. Lincare is the provider here and several offices knew of it when I called just to see how far the 'word' had traveled."*
  - [Pebblesthecorgi, Mar 2022](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/9155-medicare-coverage-of-02/) — captures the secondary-insurer caveat: *"Secondary insurers and primary insurers generally follow medicare guidelines but are not obliged to... At this point, based on the medicare ruling, the insurance company is choosing not to cover... The way is paved but the fight persists."*
  - [Dallas Denny, Jan 2024](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/6999-insurance-coverage-for-oxygen/) — confirms the knowledge-propagation lag: *"the original approval was at least a year ago so you'd think it would be common knowledge regionally by now???"*
- Supplier corporate policy refuses out-of-pocket. Note: in the [Mordecai 2026 thread](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/29481-newbie-issues-getting-oxygen-prescription-filled/), the supplier rejection was *not* on Medicare grounds (Mordecai is too young for Medicare and was paying out-of-pocket on HSA) — it was AdaptHealth corporate policy refusing CH-coded prescriptions on the basis that "insurance won't cover it" even when the patient explicitly opted out of insurance. So even with the Medicare door now open, supplier-side gatekeeping is its own distinct blocker.
- Default delivery is small E tanks, low-flow regulator, NRB
- Workaround = ladder-climbing OR welding O2

### United Kingdom — easy oxygen, demand valve gated in practice

Forum thread: [Looking to buy Cluster O2 kit for UK](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/16124-looking-to-buy-cluster-o2-kit-for-uk/) — Lymestoney (March 2024)

> "My demand valve arrived today and is working well. I got the oxygen free on prescription but opted to buy a demand valve myself. I'm just delighted to have been taken seriously by my doctor at last." — Lymestoney

> "It was £200 but it's worth it. My GP has actually now prescribed one for me for free, in addition, so I'll have a spare." — Lymestoney

Other NHS data points:
- [UK filmmaker thread](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/28957-cluster-awareness-short-film-supportfunding/): "living in the UK means I have access to NHS oxygen, which is a gamechanger"
- [UK ONB wait](https://clusterbusters.org/forums/topic/13420-a-new-breakthrough-for-me-hope-it-helps-someone-else/): "3rd block booked … a six month wait this time"

**Pattern**: oxygen + device technically reimbursed (UK is one of 8 countries per [IHS survey](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0333102416647786)). Real friction is upstream — getting the GP to take CH seriously — and second-order kit upgrades. Proposed UK chapter path: **NICE → HOOF code 18 → HOC/IHORM safety forms → home + ambulatory cylinders → demand-valve upgrade if needed.**

1. **The standard, NICE-backed prescription is the patient's first win.** [NICE CG150](https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/cg150) recommends 100% oxygen at **at least 12 L/min** via non-rebreathing mask with reservoir bag, up to 20 minutes per attack, and explicitly tells the prescriber to arrange both **home and ambulatory** oxygen. That's the floor — anything less is below NICE.
2. **HOOF Part A code 18 = cluster headache.** The [NHS England HOOF Part A](https://www.england.nhs.uk/coronavirus/wp-content/uploads/sites/52/2020/04/Home-Oxygen-Order-Form-HOOF-Part-A.pdf) lists "Cluster headache" as Primary Clinical Code 18 — the prescriber doesn't need to invent anything, the form already has a box for it. OUCH UK provides a part-completed HOOF as a download for patients to bring to a GP.
3. **Safety paperwork (IHORM + HOC) accompanies the HOOF.** Initial Home Oxygen Risk Mitigation form and Home Oxygen Consent form must be completed before BOC will install. These are friction but not a blocker.
4. **Demand valve is the upgrade question, not the entry question.** Available in principle, gated in practice:
   - Per the [OUCH UK 2022 patient leaflet](https://ouchuk.org/sites/default/files/downloads/high_flow_oxygen_therapy_2022_v8_vh.pdf), demand valve is approved for NHS prescription in England and Wales (separate HOOF form, schrader-connector cylinder). 2022 leaflet noted it was not yet available in Scotland or Northern Ireland.
   - [NHS Scotland's current guidance](https://www.rightdecisions.scot.nhs.uk/neurology-pathways/headache/9-cluster-headache-guidance/) now lists demand valve as available "for those not tolerating a non-rebreathable mask" and "can be requested when ordering oxygen" — a step up from the 2022 picture, but still positioned as a second-line option, not the default.
   - Local pathways vary. The [Nottinghamshire APC pathway](https://www.nottsapc.nhs.uk/media/xfsld5xc/cluster-headaches-home-oxygen-pathway.pdf) (last ratified Nov 2025) is one **local variation, not a UK-wide default**: it specifies an initial supply of NRB at **10–15 L/min** with a single static cylinder (the lower bound sits awkwardly below NICE's "at least 12 L/min" floor), and restricts NHS-funded demand valves to identified **chronic** patients. Chapter advice: even if your local pathway looks like Notts, ask explicitly for a NICE-compliant flow rate and for both home + ambulatory provision.
   - [OUCH UK's "we need your help" campaign](https://ouchuk.org/news/demand-valve-oxygen-we-need-your-help) confirms demand valve is treated as an "optional" in the NHS oxygen supply contract — suppliers are not obliged to provide it, which is the structural reason availability is patchy even where it's nominally approved.

So the practical friction in the UK is "you'll likely start on NRB at NICE flow rates if your GP and pathway are awake; the demand-valve upgrade depends on your region, episodic-vs-chronic status, and supplier willingness." Buying privately (£200 range, as Lymestoney did) remains a common shortcut for the demand valve specifically.

## 3. The 80/20 recommendation

### Primary: United States

- Largest addressable pool by far.
- Friction is exactly the kind of thing a guide can fix: insurance language, supplier scripts, equipment substitution tactics, the "we don't serve CH" workaround.
- Most existing forum knowledge is already US-shaped. Source material is rich.
- Sub-segments worth their own treatment (proposed US chapter structure):
  1. **Private insurance** — denial-after-correct-script, peer-to-peer appeals, "intractable" magic word, diagnosis-code precision.
  2. **Medicare / Medicare Advantage** — correct the widespread "Medicare excludes CH" belief: NCD 240.2.2 retired 2021-09-27 and 240.2 modified so MACs may cover. Hand the reader the NCD 240.2 §D / SSA §1862(a)(1)(A) citation. Note: Medicare Advantage plans must cover Original Medicare benefits and follow applicable Medicare coverage rules, but may add prior authorization/utilization management, so patients should ask the plan directly and request the denial in writing.
  3. **Self-pay / supplier route** — when insurance is more friction than it's worth (HSA, ladder-climbing to local supplier manager, the Mordecai pattern).
  4. **Caveat: Medicaid and VA** — *not yet researched in this note.* Both vary substantially. Medicaid is state-by-state with its own DME rules layered on top of the federal Medicare position; VA has its own formulary/ supply chain (CHAMPVA, community care). Each needs a separate research pass before the chapter commits to specifics.
  5. **Wrong-equipment-delivered** — logically downstream of getting a script filled, but most threads end there. Hand-off to the equipment chapter.

### Secondary: United Kingdom

- Different pattern entirely (NHS not insurance), so a separate chapter or clearly-labeled UK section.
- Chapter path: NICE → HOOF code 18 → HOC/IHORM safety forms → home + ambulatory cylinders → demand-valve upgrade if needed.
- Things to address:
  1. Getting the GP to take CH seriously and to write a NICE-compliant script (≥12 L/min, NRB+reservoir, home + ambulatory).
  2. Hand the GP HOOF Part A pre-filled with code 18 (OUCH UK provides this).
  3. Demand valve as an *upgrade*, not the entry — chronic-only in some regions, supplier-discretion in others, "buy private, get script later" as a common shortcut.
  4. Specialist referral wait times.

### Light touch: Canada + Australia/NZ

A short combined sub-section:
- "If you're in a country where a neurologist is the gatekeeper, here's how to shortcut the waitlist" handles both.
- Australia: extra weight on welding-O2 cross-reference because cost (not knowledge) is the binding constraint.

### De-prioritize: Continental EU

One paragraph:
> "In most of Germany, France, and Scandinavia, oxygen and the device are reimbursed and the prescription itself is rarely the blocker. The equipment chapter still applies if your supplier delivers a 15 LPM regulator without a demand valve."

### Out of scope

- Eastern Europe, most of Asia/LatAm/Africa: binding constraint is reimbursement + infrastructure, not patient negotiation.
